About Me

MR

I am a PhD candidate in Urban Planning and Development at the University of Southern California's Sol Price School of Public Policy, expecting to complete my degree in May 2026. My research focuses on understanding how remote work and digital technologies are reshaping urban spaces, population dynamics, and the spatial structure of cities in post-COVID America.

My dissertation, titled "Cities in the Age of Remote Work," investigates the impacts of work-from-home arrangements on urban decline and spatial structure. I work under the guidance of an exceptional committee including Elizabeth Currid-Halkett, Manuel Castells, Dowell Myers, and Jorge De La Roca.

Prior to my doctoral studies, I earned my Master of Urban Planning from USC with a concentration in Transportation Planning, and my BA in Growth and Structure of Cities from Haverford College.

Research Focus

My research lies at the intersection of urban planning, spatial economics, and demographic change. I employ quantitative methods, including spatial econometrics and causal inference, to understand how technological transformations—particularly the rise of remote work—are fundamentally altering where and how people live and work.

Current research projects include:

  • Disentangling Remote Work Effects: Examining whether urban population decline is driven by remote work policies or COVID-19 health concerns
  • Urban-Rural Development: Investigating economic growth patterns in America's heartland and challenging conventional urban-rural narratives
  • Spatial Economic Evolution: Analyzing historical Sanborn Fire Insurance maps (1888-2005) to understand long-term urban transformation
